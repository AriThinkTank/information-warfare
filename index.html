Theory 02 · Deterrence Dilution

The $500 Drone vs. the $2M Tank: Leverage Singularities

The paper predicts "deterrence dilution" — when low-cost technologies produce strategic effects disproportionate to their cost, traditional deterrence calculus collapses. The data from Ukraine and Gaza confirm this.

Cost-Effect Asymmetry: Weapon Systems in Current Conflicts (USD)
What this shows: The ratio of weapon cost (attacker) to asset destroyed (defender) — what the paper calls "leverage singularities." A $500 FPV drone can destroy a $2M+ tank, yielding a 4,000× return. Iranian Shahed-136 drones cost ~$20,000 each and have destroyed $5M+ Ukrainian air defense systems. This inverstion of the cost-effect curve is precisely what the paper predicts causes deterrence dilution.
Sources: RUSI, Oryx Project, CSIS Missile Defense Project, IISS Military Balance 2024, Reuters, Jun 2024
Drone Use in Ukraine: Monthly FPV Strikes (2023–2024)
What this shows: Exponential growth in low-cost FPV drone strikes in Ukraine validates the paper's "leverage singularity" concept. By mid-2024, drones account for more armored vehicle kills than all artillery combined.
Sources: Oryx open-source tracker, ISW, Ukrainian Armed Forces briefings, 2023–2024
Deterrence Erosion Score: Iran vs. Israel (2020–2025)
What this shows: Each escalation event (marked) erodes the credibility of previous deterrence postures. After Oct 7, after Apr 2024 drone attack, after Jun 2025 US strike on Iran — deterrence "resets" to a lower baseline, validating the paper's dilution thesis.
Sources: RAND, Carnegie Endowment, Belfer Center Harvard, CSIS, 2024–2025

Satellite Imagery as Narrative Anchor — OSINT Evidence

The paper argues that "reality anchoring" through verifiable evidence is essential to prevent narrative decay. Publicly available satellite imagery from Maxar Technologies, Planet Labs, and ESA Sentinel-2 has served as a crucial validation layer in multiple conflicts — but also as a battlefield itself, with states selectively releasing imagery to support their narratives.

Ukraine — Mariupol / Bakhmut

Maxar imagery showed systematic destruction of Mariupol (Mar–May 2022) before/after, contradicting Russian "liberation" narrative. Used by AP, NYT to anchor Ukraine's civilian casualty claims. Planet Labs imagery at Bakhmut confirmed Russian tactical gains while ISW used same imagery to show strategic stalemate.

Source: Maxar Technologies public archive · AP OSINT team · Planet Labs · Bellingcat

Gaza — Infrastructure Damage

UNOSAT (UN Satellite Centre) analysis of Sentinel-2 and commercial imagery documented 68,000+ structures damaged in Gaza (Jan 2024). Used to anchor Palestinian civilian impact narrative at UNSC. Israel disputed methodology — same imagery produced opposite conclusions in parallel information ecosystems.

Source: UNOSAT Rapid Mapping, Jan 2024 · Corey Scher/CUNY analysis · AP Visual Investigation

Operation Sindoor — Satellite Verification

Commercial satellite imagery from Maxar (released via Reuters) confirmed structural damage at claimed strike coordinates within 24 hours, giving India's narrative a strong factual substrate. Pakistan's competing narrative lacked corresponding satellite validation — confirming paper's "fact-ambiguity ratio" as determinant of narrative longevity.

Theory 06 · The Underdog Paradox & Narrative Advantage

Low Capacity, High Narrative Return: How Non-State Actors Win Information Wars

The paper predicts technological diffusion disproportionately benefits actors with lesser conventional capacity. The data confirms this — Hamas, Houthis, and non-state proxies show higher narrative ROI than conventional powers.

Narrative ROI: Conventional vs. Non-State Actors (Information Warfare Investment vs. Strategic Reach)
What this shows: Non-state and smaller actors achieve dramatically higher "narrative ROI" — strategic influence per dollar of information warfare investment. Hamas's $50M total Oct 7 operation achieved global narrative shift worth billions. Houthis' social media campaign for Red Sea operations cost <$1M, disrupted $200B+ in global shipping. This is the Underdog Paradox quantified.
Sources: RAND, Carnegie, Reuters, Bloomberg estimates, CSIS · All figures are public estimates
Technology Diffusion: Tools Available to Non-State Actors (2015–2025)
What this shows: The dramatic drop in cost and rise in accessibility of key information warfare and military technologies — FPV drones, deepfake tools, social media amplification — over the past decade confirms the paper's "cognitive battlefield flattening" prediction. What required nation-state resources in 2015 is now available for <$10,000 in 2025.
